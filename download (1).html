      Learn to understand the I do, we do, you do structure of Lambda school curriculum and how it is valuable for learning.

      Difference Between Traditional Grades and Outcomes Based Assessment

      In a standard school grading system a percentage is calculated and you earn a letter grade. There are some challenges with this system that makes it ineffective when training a technical employee. In one scenario you have a straight âAâ student who just naturally gets the material and they meet the outcomes but arenât pushed or motivated to work up to their own potential. Then you have a âCâ student who is only meeting about 70% of the outcomes but just keeps continuing on to half understand material in the next topic. Finally, youâve got a student that is doing the bare minimum and getting the 59.5% required to not have to redo the class. They are getting some credit for showing up and probably not meeting many of the outcomes. All these students graduate with the same high school diploma.

      This system doesnât work in the real world. An employer wants to know that you can do the tasks given to you at near 100% accuracy. They are looking for a motivated individual that will go beyond the bare minimum requirements. The employer wants to be able to train you quickly on new technologies, so your ability to learn matters. Finally, they want you to be able to take critical feedback and use it to improve. If youâve always been the straight âAâ student, critical feedback can be crushing and demoralizing.

      Lambda Assessment Scale

      At Lambda school we use a standardized scale of 1, 2 and 3.

      1 - Does Not Meet Expectations

      2 - Meets Expectations

      This means that you met all the outcome expectations of the project. This is the level you are aiming for at Lambda school. It means you understand the material and are ready to move on.

      3 - Exceeds Expectations

      This is a âspecialâ category for those that are able to go above and beyond the outcome expectations of the project. For those of you use to earning âAâsâ, this is not equivalent. This is for stretch goals that have been met because you find this particular project easy and had time to do a lot of extra work.

      Benefits of Outcome Based Assessment

      Clarity

      The focus on outcomes creates a clear expectation of what needs to be accomplished by the end of the lesson. Students will understand what is expected of them and instructors will know what they need to teach during the course. Clarity is important in strategic programs like Lambda school where there are many teams working across a track. Each team member has a clear understanding of what needs to be accomplished in each sprint. Those designing and planning the curriculum are expected to work backwards once an outcome has been decided upon; they must determine what knowledge and skills will be required to reach the outcome.

      Flexibility

      With a clear sense of what needs to be accomplished, instructors will be able to structure their lessons around the studentâs needs. Instructors are free to teach their students using any method. Instructors will also be able to recognize diversity among students by using various teaching and assessment techniques during their class. Outcomes based assessment is a student-centered learning model. Instructors guide and help the students understand the material in any way necessary. Group work is one of the methods instructors can use to help students learning.

      Comparison

      The articulated outcomes allow Lambda school to assess the studentâs achievements rapidly, leading to increased amounts of feedback to students. These outcomes also work for school to work transitions. A potential employer can find what outcomes a Lambda school student has achieved. They can then determine if the potential employee has the skills necessary for the job. At Lambda school the entire focus is on meeting employers expectations for educational outcomes.

      Involvement

      Student involvement in the classroom is a key part of outcomes based assessment. Students are expected to do their own learning, so that they gain a full understanding of the material. Increased student involvement allows students to feel responsible for their own learning, and they should learn more through this individual learning. Employer partners are asked to give input to Lambda School to uphold the standards of education and to ensure that students will be prepared for employment after school.

      The Lambda school tracks follow a specific curriculum with the framework of I do, You do, We do. All courses have a universal schedule with four units of focus track, eight sprints of Labs, two units of track-specific curriculum, and then Lambda X. Each unit consists of four sprints with the last sprint being a Project/Build Week.

      A sprint is a timeboxed iteration of a continuous development cycle. Within a Sprint, the team completes a planned amount of work to prepare for a review. The term Sprint comes from the Scrum Agile method but the basic idea of Kanban continuous delivery is also in the essence of Sprint Scrum. At Lambda a sprint is one week for a full-time cohort and two weeks for part-time.

      There are four units in the Core Curriculum which correspond with 4 build weeks.
      Labs consist of eight sprints which give you time to polish your project with your team and make it a central portfolio piece. Lambda X consists of eight sprints per iteration. This time allows students to continue polishing their portfolio pieces. This ensures they are ready to move on to employment.

      Academic Expectations

      The Lambda school assessment process will state when you have achieved mastery of each goal and act as a signal for when you need extra support. Lambda is based on objective mastery and not grades. Future employers want you to know how to perform certain tasks and we donât waste time on unnecessary concepts. For this reason, regular assessment is a cornerstone of our educational program.

      Module Assessment

      Daily 1:1 Review

      You will meet with your assigned Team Lead daily for 10 minutes. Besides providing you with a verbal code review, your TL will work with you to assess your understanding of the current module. Your TL will indicate if you need extra support to master the objectives. If indicated by your TL, you will be required to attend two hours of TL Hour to target the objectives you need more time to understand.

      Sprint Assessment

      Sprint 1:1 Review

      Following the completion of your sprint challenge, you will meet with your Team Lead to discuss your performance. The Team Lead will submit a review of your Sprint Challenge as well as verification of performance in the following areas;

      • Technical skills
      • Teamwork/Collaboration skills
      • Career Readiness

      Students who are unable to meet the objectives on any of the evaluation points above will be required to attend Support Hours for that sprint.

      Unit Assessment

      Sprint Progression

      Students will attend Targeted Support hours, also repeating units as needed. Every student stays within the same sprint group with instructor-led guided projects. Support Hours and content repetition are available during the Core Track Curriculum, Labs, and during the Additional Track Curriculum

      Accumulation of two sprint challenges rated at a 1 will show if you need to repeat the unit.
